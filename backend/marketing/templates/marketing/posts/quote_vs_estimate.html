If you've been working in the trades for any amount of time, you've probably heard the words "quote" and "estimate" used interchangeably. But did you know they have entirely different legal and practical meanings? Understanding the difference can save you from losing money or alienating clients.

What is an Estimate?

An estimate is exactly what it sounds like: a rough guess of how much a job will cost. It is not legally binding.

You provide an estimate when you don't have all the information yet. For example, if a client calls you and says they want a kitchen remodel but haven't chosen the cabinets or flooring yet, you can give them an estimate based on your past experience with similar kitchens. If the final price ends up being 20% higher because they chose expensive marble countertops, that's perfectly fine—it was just an estimate.

What is a Quotation?

A quotation (or quote) is a fixed price offer. Once a client accepts a quote, it is legally binding.

You should only provide a quote when you know exactly what the job entails. The client has chosen the materials, you have inspected the site, and you know exactly how many hours of labor it will take. If you quote $5,000 for a job and it ends up costing you $6,000 because you miscalculated the amount of paint needed, you have to eat that $1,000 loss. You cannot charge the client more than the quoted amount unless they request additional work (which requires a Change Order).

When to Use Which?

  • Use an Estimate during the early stages of a project to see if the client's budget aligns with your pricing.
  • Use a Quote when the project scope is finalized and you are ready to sign a contract and begin work.
