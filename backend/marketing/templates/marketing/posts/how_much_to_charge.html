One of the most common reasons contracting businesses fail isn't a lack of skill—it's poor pricing. If you charge too much, you lose bids. If you charge too little, you work yourself to exhaustion and still go out of business.

The Two Main Pricing Models

1. Hourly Rates (Time and Materials)

In this model, you charge the client for the exact cost of the materials, plus an hourly rate for the labor. This is great for repair work or jobs where the scope is unknown.

Pros: You are guaranteed to get paid for every hour you work. You never lose money if the job takes longer than expected.

Cons: Clients often hate this because they don't know the final price upfront. It also penalizes you for working efficiently; if you invest in a better tool that cuts your time in half, you make half as much money.

2. Fixed Pricing

In this model, you give the client one flat price for the entire job. This requires you to estimate the materials and labor accurately beforehand.

Pros: Clients love predictability. Furthermore, if you finish the job faster than expected, your effective hourly rate skyrockets.

Cons: If you underestimate the job, you lose money.

How to Calculate Your Rate

Never just "guess" a number or charge what the guy down the street is charging. You need to calculate your true costs:

  1. Direct Labor: How much do you pay your employees (or yourself) per hour? Don't forget taxes and insurance.
  2. Materials: The exact cost of the supplies.
  3. Overhead: This is the silent killer. You must add a percentage to cover your truck payments, gas, insurance, accounting software, and advertising.
  4. Profit Margin: After all expenses are paid, your business needs to make a profit to grow. A common profit margin for contractors is 15-20%.
